## Case 2: Acute Pancreatitis (68474)

### Key Findings
- **Fat stranding** centered on the pancreas, along with **pancreatic thickening and edema**
- **Fluid in the anterior pararenal space** tracking along anterior pararenal space bilaterally, with no mature organized collection
- **Small volume splenic vein thrombosis** but no other acute complication

**Anterior pararenal space contains pancreas, ascending and descending colon, and duodenum. Fluid predominantly in this space (rather than intraperitoneal) should make you think about anterior pararenal source, most commonly the pancreas.**

**Other Findings:**
- Cholelithiasis
- Reactive thickening of colon
- **Hepatic steatosis** - liver attenuation is >25 HU less than spleen on portal venous phase

Findings are consistent with acute pancreatitis with small splenic vein thrombosis.

### Learning Material: Diagnosis of Acute Pancreatitis
Acute Pancreatitis can be **diagnosed on the basis of 2/3 of:**
- Typical history
- Elevated lipase (>3x upper limit)
- Imaging findings

In most situations in emergency settings, imaging is not required for a diagnosis.
In the acute setting, the most common reason to image is to help determine the etiology (i.e. **ultrasound to assess for gallstones**).
CT is most often performed to **look for complications** of pancreatitis.

### Complications
On CT, you should be looking for the following complications:
- **Necrosis** - determines if interstitial edematous pancreatitis or necrotizing pancreatitis
- **Fluid collections**
- **Vascular complications** (venous thrombosis, arterial pseudoaneurysms)

## Case 3: Necrotizing Pancreatitis (81542)

### Key Findings
- Intrapancreatic necrosis (areas of **non-enhancing pancreatic parenchyma**). Compare enhancing parenchyma vs non-enhancing necrotic parenchyma.
- **Splenic Vein Thrombosis**
- Ascites and **developing collections** ("acute necrotic collections"). Notice lack of significant rim-enhancement, suggesting not yet mature/organized.

### Learning Material:
This is an example of **necrotizing pancreatitis**, with intrapancreatic necrosis.
In addition to a portal venous phase acquisition, a pancreatic protocol CT includes a **late arterial phase**, where there is peak pancreatic parenchymal enhancement. In pancreatitis, this helps us identify intrapancreatic necrosis (parts of the pancreatic parenchyma that are non-enhancing).

### Complications of Pancreatitis on CT
On CT, we are looking for the following complications routinely:
- Necrosis
- Fluid collections (more on naming below)
- Vascular complications (venous thrombosis, pseudoaneurysm)

This case is a good example of intrapancreatic necrosis. There are also early developing collections (acute necrotic collections) and a vascular complication in splenic vein thrombosis.

**Necrosis:**
Pancreatitis is classified as either: interstitial edematous pancreatitis (IEP) OR necrotizing pancreatitis based on whether necrosis is present.
- Necrotizing pancreatitis is defined by either intrapancreatic necrosis OR peripancreatic necrosis.
- The greater the intrapancreatic necrosis, the worse the prognosis.

**Naming Fluid Collections:**
Fluid collections are named differently depending on if IEP or necrotizing pancreatitis. This is not just semantics, the naming reflects the contents (more simple fluid in IEP vs more necrotic solid gunk if necrotizing) -- and this affects how the collections are managed.

**IEP:**
- Acute (<4 weeks) = **Acute peripancreatic fluid collection**
- Chronic (>4 weeks) = **Pseudocyst**

**Necrotizing Pancreatitis:**
- Acute (<4 weeks) = **Acute Necrotic Collection**
- Chronic (>4 weeks) = **Walled off Necrosis**

## Case 4: Necrotizing Pancreatitis, Pseudoaneurysm (92310)

### Key Findings
- Necrotizing pancreatitis with non-enhancing parenchyma
- **Fluid collection:** Pancreatic and peri-pancreatic collection with **rim enhancement** and **containing gas**, representing infected walled off necrosis. Malecot drain in place.
- **Hemorrhagic material** in the collection (note high attenuation material on non-contrast acquisition)
- **Large pseudoaneurysm** (see arterial phase and venous phase images)

### Basic Principle: Pseudoaneurysm vs Active Extravasation

**Pseudoaneurysm:**
- Arterial phase: Fills with IV contrast, matching attenuation of arterial blood pool
- Maintains morphology on venous phase

**Active extravasation:**
- Arterial phase: Extravascular contrast (any shape, may be linear with shooting blood), matching attenuation of arterial blood pool
- Venous phase: changes in morphology, usually expanding

## Case 5: Acute Cholecystitis (14532)

### Key Findings
- **Distended gallbladder** (> 4 cm diameter, "tense" appearance)
- Gallstones, including calcified stone at neck
- Mural/**wall thickening**
- **Inflammatory stranding/fluid** centred on the gallbladder
- Mild **hyperemia in adjacent liver** parenchyma

**Abdominal ultrasound is most commonly the first imaging modality to assess for acute cholecystitis, and has advantages over CT.** But, in patients with nonspecific pain you may see cholecystitis on CT.

### Acute Calculous Cholecystitis
Acute cholecystitis simply means acute inflammation of the gallbladder. The most common cause by far is cholelithiasis/gallstones. So most acute cholecystitis is acute calculous cholecystitis.

**Pathophysiology:** Gallstone obstructs the neck or cystic duct, the gallbladder distends and becomes inflamed +/- infected.
- Presence of gallstones
- Gallbladder distension (> 4 cm in diameter, but more importantly "tense" appearance)
- Positive sonographic murphy's sign
- "Secondary findings" of cholecystitis (wall thickening > 3 mm, adjacent inflammation)

### Complications of Cholecystitis
- **Gangrenous cholecystitis** - areas of non-enhancing wall
- **Perforation** - wall defect, pericholecystic fluid collections/abscess
- **Emphysematous cholecystitis** - gas in gallbladder wall
- **Cholecystoenteric fistula** - fistula between gallbladder and small bowel
- **Gallbladder hemorrhage** +/- cystic artery pseudoaneurysm

## Case 6: Gangrenous Cholecystitis (40321)

### Key Findings:
- Findings of **acute cholecystitis** (distended gallbladder, gallstones, wall thickening, adjacent inflammation)
- **Multifocal areas of non-enhancing gallbladder mucosa** - findings consistent with gangrenous cholecystitis

### Learning Material:
**Gangrenous cholecystitis** is a complication of acute cholecystitis, referring to **gallbladder wall necrosis**. Risk factors include diabetes, male sex, cardiovascular disease, high WBC count. It has a higher mortality than uncomplicated cholecystitis.

**Imaging Findings:**
- Interrupted wall enhancement (multifocal areas of non-enhancement)
- May see "floating intraluminal membranes"
- Perfusion defects on doppler ultrasound
- Sonographic murphy's sign may be absent (ischemia leads to denervation of gallbladder)

**Basic Concept: Gallbladder Thickening**
Not all gallbladder wall thickening is acute cholecystitis. Other causes:
- Secondary thickening due to primary hepatic inflammation
- Systemic processes (cirrhosis, renal disease, low albumin, etc.)
- Gallbladder can also be distended in patients who have not eaten

## Case 7: Perforated Cholecystitis with Intrahepatic Abscess (86590)

### Key Findings
- **Distended gallbladder**, stones better seen on ultrasound
- **Gallbladder wall defect** at site of perforation, communicating with large **intrahepatic abscess in segment 5** (measures 7.1 x 4.6 cm, multiloculated)
- Seeing a **wall defect** or pericholecystic collection should suggest gallbladder perforation.

### Learning Material:
**Gallbladder perforation** is a complication of acute cholecystitis. With tense distension of the gallbladder, you can eventually get necrosis of gallbladder wall and perforation.
When the gallbladder perforates:
- May spill freely into peritoneal cavity
- There may be pericholecystic abscesses/collections (intrahepatic or subhepatic)
- A fistula may develop with adjacent bowel
- In this case, it resulted in an intrahepatic abscess

## Case 8: Hemorrhage with pseudoaneurysm post chole (01629)

### Key Findings
- Two cholecystostomy tubes present, related to known prior perforated cholecystitis. Gallbladder overall well decompressed, ongoing surrounding inflammation
- **Small hyperattenuating ovoid structure in the gallbladder lumen** on venous phase. On **arterial phase, this matches arterial bloodpool**
- Findings are in keeping with a **pseudoaneurysm**, which is causing the acute bleeding draining via chole tube.

### Learning Material
Pseudoaneurysm of the cystic artery due to cholecystitis or post-intervention is a rare but important complication. Consider this in patients with blood draining from chole tube, drop in hemoglobin, or hematoma on imaging.

**Important principle:** Gallstones are common and are commonly ovoid hyperattenuating structures. Gallstones should be the same attenuation on non-contrast, arterial, and delayed phase. **In contrast, to confirm a pseudoaneurysm it should match bloodpool.**

**Pseudoaneurysm vs Active Extravasation:**
- Pseudoaneurysm - ovoid, matches blood pool, morphology maintained on venous phase (contained)
- Active extravasation - morphology changes/expands on venous/delayed phase (not contained)

## Case 9: Choledocholithiasis with Ascending Cholangitis (11389)

### Key Findings
- **Biliary dilatation** (both intrahepatic and extrahepatic)
- **Cholelithiasis** (calcified gallstones)
- **Biliary obstruction caused by choledocholithiasis** (stone in the distal common duct). Stones sometimes easier to see on non-contrast acquisition.
- **Findings of biliary inflammation/cholangitis** - mild biliary wall enhancement and peri-biliary perfusional changes on arterial phase
- **Gallbladder is also distended** with stone at neck and trace thickening, with areas of interrupted/poor mural enhancement

Overall, findings are in keeping with choledocholithiasis with biliary obstruction, with imaging findings suggesting ascending cholangitis. There is associated acute cholecystitis with likely gangrenous change.

### Learning Material:
**Choledocholithiasis** refers to stones in the common duct.
**Acute Cholangitis** ("ascending cholangitis") refers to bacterial infection of the biliary tree, often the result of obstruction. Ascending cholangitis has a high mortality and needs to be decompressed.

**With imaging, when there is clinical suspicion for acute cholangitis, answer three questions:**
1. Is there biliary obstruction?
2. What is the cause of biliary obstruction?
3. Are there features supportive of cholangitis/inflammation?

**Basic principle: Common duct dilatation**
- A normal common duct measures up to 6 mm + 1 mm for every decade above 60
- If larger, need to look carefully for an obstructing cause
- After cholecystectomy, diameter often increases (often up to 12 mm)

## Case 10: Gallstone Ileus (19079)

### Key Findings
- **Small bowel obstruction** (dilated proximal bowel loops to 4 cm, abrupt transition point, right lower quadrant, bowel loops collapsed distal to this)
- **Partially calcified gallstone** in the lumen at site of obstruction
- **Pneumobilia** (gas in biliary system - central and can be followed into the CBD)
- Direct visualization of **cholecystoduodenal fistula** (fistula between gallbladder and duodenum). With oral contrast - with contrast in the gallbladder directly contiguous with duodenum.

### Learning Material
Gallstone "ileus" is a misnomer -- it refers to a small bowel obstruction caused by a gallstone. The gallstone gets into the GI tract via fistula between gallbladder and bowel (i.e. cholecystoduodenal fistula). Fistula forms due to chronic inflammation in the gallbladder.

**Classic Rigler's triad on Xray:** small bowel obstruction, right lower quadrant calcified gallstone, and pneumobilia.

**Other Related Conditions:**
- **Bouveret syndrome** - same process, but stone obstructs more proximally (stomach/duodenum)
- **Mirizzi Syndrome** - gallstone impacted in gallbladder neck/cystic duct causing biliary tree obstruction with biliary dilatation

## Case 11: Hepatic Abscess (59161)

### Key Findings
- **Large fluid collections** (low attenuation centrally), largest one occupying right liver
- Peripheral **rim of enhancement**
- **"Clustered" appearance** of multiple purulent areas that coalesce into one - classic for pyogenic abscesses
- **Reactive change** in surrounding liver
- Findings of **diverticulitis** (the likely cause of the abscesses -- dissemination via portal vein)

Here is another abscess with the same imaging features. The clustered appearance, fluid attenuation with thin well defined rim, and changes in the adjacent liver parenchyma all in keeping with an abscess over a tumor.

### Learning Material:
An "abscess" is a collection of pus/inflammatory tissue, caused by infection. On imaging, you will see a collection of fluid with peripheral enhancement, positive mass effect, and associated inflammation. Liver abscess is the most common solid organ abscess, presenting with fever and RUQ pain.

**Most common causes:** biliary infection (ascending cholangitis), GI infection (diverticulitis, appendicitis). Organisms are most commonly E coli, Klebsiella.

**Other Findings to look for:**
- On arterial phase, may see hyperenhancement surrounding abscess
- Gas in the abscess (related to intervention or gas-forming organism)
- Thrombophlebitis (hepatic or portal vein thrombosis)
- Rupture

**Treatment is antibiotics, percutaneous drainage.**

**Other types of Hepatic Abscess:**
1. **Amebic** abscess (parasite) - often unilocular, round, thick walled
2. **Echinococcal** (hydatid disease; parasite) - cystic lesion with multiple layers, "daughter cysts"
3. **Candidiasis** - typically appear as microabscesses in the liver and spleen

## Case 12: Acute Uncomplicated Appendicitis (86658)

### Key Findings
- Appendix is fluid filled and **distended to 11 mm** with thickening
- **Surrounding inflammatory changes**, centered on the appendix and involving the **mesoappendix**
- **Reactive thickening of adjacent small bowel**
- No appendicolith
- **No** mural defect, periappendiceal collections, extramural gas to suggest perforation

Findings are consistent with **acute uncomplicated appendicitis**. Based on clinical factors and lack of appendicolith, the patient was treated non-operatively.

### Learning Material
**Key points about appendicitis:**
- Size criteria: "Greater than or equal to 7 mm" is based on ULTRASOUND measurements. **On CT, a normal appendix commonly measures > 7 mm!** When it does though, you need to look very closely for other findings of appendicitis.
- When you see inflammation, think "where is it centered?"
- Gas in appendix often seen in normal appendix (non-obstructed at the base)

**CT Findings:**
- Dilated appendix (7 mm is not an absolute cutoff)
- Appendix often fluid filled
- Thick wall
- Peri-appendiceal inflammation
- **Appendicolith** -- must report if present, as impacts management
- Focal cecal thickening (cecal bar sign)

**Complications to look for:**
- **Perforation** (extraluminal gas, discontinuous wall, adjacent fluid collection)
- **Abscess** (local or distant in liver)
- **Thrombophlebitis** (SMV, portal vein thrombus)
- Fistula (rare)

**Basic Skill: Finding the Appendix**
1. Follow the colon from rectum to cecum
2. Identify the terminal ileum and ileocecal valve
3. Appendix arises from the cecum on the SAME SIDE of the IC valve
4. Follow the appendix to prove that it is blind ended
5. Practice finding the appendix on routine cases

## Case 13: Perforated Appendicitis with Collections (55888)

### Key Findings
- **Expected site of appendix markedly abnormal**
- Severe **inflammatory changes** in the RLQ centred on this
- Multiple fluid collections associated, **up to 5.8 x 3.3 cm** with rim enhancement and positive mass effect on surrounding structures
- Additional collections are seen
- Collections are **inseparable from right adnexa**
- Severe **reactive thickening of bowel loops adjacent**

Findings are in keeping with **perforated appendicitis with multiple fluid collections/abscesses** in the right lower quadrant.

### Learning Material
**CT signs that mean perforated appendicitis:**
- Highly specific: extraluminal gas, periappendiceal abscess/loculated collection, extraluminal appendicolith, focal mural enhancement defect ("hole sign")
- Supportive: phlegmon, marked fat stranding, free fluid/ileus, often larger diameter

**Accuracy:** Abscess or extraluminal gas: very specific (~98-99%) but modest sensitivity (~34-53%)

**How to report:** Impression: "Acute perforated appendicitis" when you see any specific sign. Describe: appendix visibility/diameter, wall defect, collection size/location, gas content, and complications.

**Practice tips:** Sometimes the appendix "blows up" -- if appendix not seen but there's a RLQ abscess/phlegmon at the expected location, perforation is likely.

**Management:** With well formed collections, patients usually get IV antibiotics and percutaneous drainage before surgery (rather than going right to surgery). Delayed/interval appendectomy after sepsis control is often safer and easier.

## Case 15: Epiploic Appendagitis (Right) (35569)

### Key Findings
- **Ovoid fat-attenuation lesion** associated with the colon, thin high density rim
- **Surrounding inflammation**
- **Central dot sign** occasionally seen (small dot representing thrombosed feeding vein)

If chronic, torsed epiploic appendage can become a "loose peritoneal body" that may calcify and be mobile.

### Learning Material
**"Epiploic Appendagitis"** refers to inflammation/infarction of an epiploic appendage (fatty structures arising from the serosal surface of colon). It is **self-limiting** and can be managed with **NSAIDs**. It is most important as a mimic of other pathology (appendicitis or diverticulitis).

**Epiploic appendagitis vs Omental infarct:**
- Epiploic appendagitis: LLQ > RLQ, central dot sign, relatively small (1-3cm)
- Omental infarct: RLQ most common, no central dot sign, usually larger area (>3 cm)

**Both are benign and treated conservatively.**

Umbrella term: **"intraperitoneal focal fat infarction"** refers to these benign conditions: epiploic appendagitis, omental infarction, and perigastric appendagitis.

## Case 17: Perigastric Appendagitis (17263)

### Key Findings
- **Ovoid structure in the gastrohepatic ligament** along lesser curvature of stomach
- Associated **significant submucosal thickening of gastric wall along lesser curvature**
- Ovoid structure contains bulk fat with surrounding **inflammation/stranding**

Findings are consistent with **perigastric appendagitis**. Although more rare than epiploic appendagitis, this represents another "intraperitoneal focal fat infarction".

### Learning Material
**Intraperitoneal focal fat infarction** are benign/self limiting inflammation of fat:
- Epiploic Appendagitis
- Omental Infarct
- Perigastric Appendagitis

Perigastric appendagitis is rare but can occur in gastrohepatic ligament or elsewhere around the stomach. Be careful to not overcall it when there is other more serious pathology (perforated ulcer, adjacent malignancy, etc).

## Case 18: Omental Infarct (62599)

### Key Findings
- **Stranding in the RLQ omentum**. The anatomy is key - this is in the omentum (not an epiploic appendage).
- No acute inflammatory process in the adjacent structures causing the stranding
- **Swirling of omental vessels** -- that you can trace superiorly to the rest of the greater omentum

Usually, omental infarcts are larger and more conspicuous than on this case.

### Learning Material
**Omental infarction** is a type of "focal fat infarction" in the omentum caused by vascular compromise, possibly due to twisting of the omentum in the right lower quadrant. It is managed conservatively.

**Omental infarct vs Epiploic appendagitis:**
- Omental infarct: RLQ most common, no central dot sign, usually larger area (>3 cm)
- Epiploic appendagitis: LLQ > RLQ, central dot sign, relatively small (1-3cm)

Both are benign and treated conservatively. Umbrella term: **intraperitoneal focal fat infarction**.

## Case 19: Crohn's with Active Ileal Inflammation (91642)

### Key Findings
- **Terminal ileal thickening** with **mural hyperenhancement** (compare mural attenuation with other loops of small bowel). Follow the distal ileum - a moderate length of ileum is involved. These findings indicate **active ileal inflammation**.
- Other findings of active inflammation: ulceration, **vascular engorgement/comb sign**, adjacent edema
- **Asymmetric** mural hyperenhancement is relatively **specific** for Crohn's

Other small bowel imaging findings in Crohn's disease are covered elsewhere (stricture, penetrating disease). For now, remember that ileitis is on the differential for RLQ pain, and know the signs for active inflammation in Crohn's ileitis.

When you see small bowel thickening, the attenuation of the wall and distribution can help with the differential diagnosis.

## Case 20: Crohn's with Stricturing (14581)

### Key Findings and Learning Material
When looking at CT Enterography in Crohn's disease, you should specifically look for each of the following in the small bowel on every case: **active inflammation, strictures, and penetrating disease.**

**Active inflammation:**
- **Mural hyperenhancement** (compare with normal loops)
- Thickening of involved loops, which can be **asymmetric** (specific in Crohn's, mesenteric side > antimesenteric)
- Perienteric stranding
- **Mesenteric engorgement**

**Stricture:** **luminal narrowing (>50%)** with **upstream dilatation > 2.5 cm**. Often associated with active inflammation. There are some situations where upstream small bowel will not be >2.5 cm, but we are still confident there is a stricture (multiple strictures, or penetrating disease decompressing upstream).

**Penetrating Disease:** No significant penetrating disease on this case. Findings to look out for include: Fistulas (simple, complex), inflammatory mass, abscess, perforation.

**Extraintestinal manifestations** should also be assessed for: sacroiliitis, gallstones, peri-anal disease/abscess, etc.

## Case 21: Crohn's with Penetrating Disease (24995)

### Key Findings
- **Acute inflammation terminal and distal ileum**: stratified mural hyperenhancement, thickening, mural edema, engorged vasa recta with comb sign, stranding
- Mild associated TI stricturing with **luminal narrowing** of the TI with upstream dilatation over short segment. Given the degree of inflammation, the degree of stricturing is likely mild
- **Penetrating Disease:** inflammatory mass in the right lower quadrant mesentery, and separately a small developing collection/abscess that contains gas; with complex fistulous disease including multiple likely enteroenteric fistulae

### Learning Material
**Key Concept: Penetrating Disease in Crohn's -- Definitions:**
- **Sinus Tract** - blind-ending tract extending beyond serosa
- **Simple Fistula** - single tract connecting bowel lumen to another epithelialized surface (named based on what they connect -- i.e. enteroenteric fistula)
- **Complex Fistula** - more than one fistulous tract. Can present with an 'asterisk-shaped' appearance in the mesentery
- **Inflammatory Mass** - dense mesenteric inflammation without a fluid component or wall
- **Abscess** - fluid collection with rim enhancement (wall)
- **Free perforation** - rare, but can occur

## Case 24: Bilateral TOAs (34609)

### Case findings
- Extensive **inflammatory change/fluid** centred in the low pelvis, extending superiorly in **both flanks**
- **Rim-enhancing fluid collections in both adnexae** (right more confluent, with tubular fluid collection on the left) - replacing both adnexae, unable to discern ovary from tube
- Reactive inflammation of adjacent pelvic structures

### Other imaging findings in PID to be aware of:
- **Liver capsule inflammation** (Fitz-Hugh-Curtis syndrome) - perihepatic inflammation
- **Salpingitis** - thickened tubes (>5 mm), hypervascular on ultrasound
- Reactive thickening of adjacent planes and organs in the pelvis

### Learning Material
**Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID)** refers to infection of the upper female genital tract. Most common causes are chlamydia, gonorrhea. As the infection ascends, it can cause cervicitis, **endometritis**, and then infect adnexal structures. Infection usually progresses from salpingitis (tubal inflammation), pyosalpinx (distended tube filled with purulent material), and then to **tubo-ovarian abscess (TOA)**.

## Case 25: Obstructing Ureteric Stone (07936)

### Key Findings
- Mild to moderate **left hydronephrosis**. Left kidney is also larger and lower attenuation than the right as a result
- Associated stranding/fluid surrounding left kidney in perinephric space
- **3 mm obstructing stone** left distal ureter
- Hepatic steatosis

### Learning Material
Renal colic studies are performed **without IV contrast**, which makes small calculi more conspicuous.

**Practical points:**
- First look for findings of obstruction, and then follow the ureter to look for the cause
- Differentiate **phleboliths** (calcifications related to small veins) and ureteric stones by location and appearance (**"soft tissue rim"** sign around stones)
- Keep an eye out for obstructing non-calcified lesions (soft tissue lesion, blood clot, extrinsic compression)

## Case 26: Pyelonephritis (75224)

### Key Findings
- Bilateral kidneys demonstrate **striated nephrogram** enhancement pattern, right greater than left. Notice linear/wedge shaped bands of reduced enhancement in a radial pattern
- **Perinephric stranding**, right greater than left
- Some urothelial thickening and inflammation along proximal ureter
- No abscess, no hydronephrosis or obstructing stone

### Learning Material
Patient presented with fever and right flank pain with CVA tenderness. Pyelonephritis is a clinical diagnosis. **CT is commonly performed to assess for complications, but not infrequently you will be the first to suggest the diagnosis.**

**CT Imaging Findings in Pyelonephritis:**
- Perinephric stranding/fluid
- Altered enhancement pattern (classically striated nephrogram)
- Urothelial thickening/enhancement
- Complications: **abscess** (intra or perirenal), **renal vein thrombosis**, **emphysematous pyelonephritis** (gas in parenchyma - surgical emergency), **pyonephrosis**

**Basic Principle: Enhancement patterns of the kidney:**
- **Striated nephrogram** (alternating low and normal enhancing bands, radially arranged): unilateral = acute pyelonephritis, ureteric obstruction, renal vein thrombosis; bilateral = acute pyelonephritis, tubular obstruction, hypotension

## Case 27: XGP (84637)

### Key Findings
- **Staghorn calculus** present in vast majority
- **"Bear Paw Sign"** - low attenuation rounded spaces relating to very dilated calyces, but replacing parenchyma with thin surrounding parenchyma. Notice that the renal pelvis is paradoxically contracted in comparison
- Mild perinephric stranding
- Findings are diffuse (can be focal/only involve part of the kidney)
- Findings are largely confined to kidney, but can extend into perirenal fat or beyond

### Learning Material
**XGP (Xanthogranulomatous Pyelonephritis)** is a chronic granulomatous pyelonephritis. In XGP, the kidney is eventually replaced by reactive/inflammatory tissue and becomes non-functional. **Treatment is nephrectomy.** The imaging appearance is specific so is important to know.

## Case 28: Perforated Gastric Ulcer (55190)

### Key Findings
- **Free intraperitoneal gas** (antidependent, some locules gastrohepatic ligament near site of perforation)
- **Gastric ulcer**. Scroll through it and notice that the fluid within is contiguous with extraluminal fluid - a mural defect
- **Distal gastric thickening** also noted
- Moderate volume ascites
- **Peritoneal thickening and enhancement**, suggesting peritoneal irritation/peritonitis

### Learning Material
The key finding to never miss is **free intraperitoneal gas**. Gas where it shouldn't be can either be from inside (bowel), outside (surgery/intervention), or infection. **Free intraperitoneal gas should be considered perforated viscus until proven otherwise** (unless immediate post-surgical). In this case, the source is the stomach. Even if you do not identify the source, the patient needs to be taken to emergency surgery.

## Case 29: Diverticulitis with Localized Perforation (94608)

### Key Findings
- **Proximal transverse colonic thickening**
- **Inflammatory stranding** surrounding a colonic diverticulum/thickened colon
- **Extraluminal locules of gas** adjacent (localized/microperforation)
- No distant free gas/frank pneumoperitoneum
- Small volume free fluid
- No mature drainable fluid collection/abscess
- Normal appendix

### Learning Material
A wider window (**lung window**) helps identify gas. Patients with diverticulitis and **free perforation** (pneumoperitoneum, distant free gas, findings of peritonitis) require emergency surgery. In this case, the locules of free gas are localized -- depending on the patient they may be treated with IV antibiotics and supportive care.

## Case 30: Extensive Bowel Ischemia/Infarction (69856)

### Key Findings
- **Portal venous gas**. In the liver, this will be gas that is more peripheral and branching. In contrast, pneumobilia is often more central. In this case, there is also large volume gas in the portal venous system (including SMV branches)
- **Frank pneumatosis** involving small bowel and colon. More conspicuous on lung window
- **Non-enhancing bowel loops** -- compare (no wall enhancement) vs other loops
- **Pneumoperitoneum** (free gas) - either from perforation or gas tracking from pneumatosis

### Learning Material
This is a case of **very extensive frank bowel infarction**.

**Imaging findings in acute mesenteric ischemia:**
- **Bowel wall thickening** - nonspecific, more common in venous ischemia
- **Mural hyperenhancement** - nonspecific
- **Mural non-enhancement** - specific finding suggesting bowel infarction
- **Pneumatosis, portal venous gas** - should suggest ischemia/infarction until proven otherwise
- Mesenteric stranding, ascites
- **Acute vascular findings** - venous thrombosis, arterial occlusion - specific

**Most important Causes of Pneumatosis:**
- Bowel ischemia/infarction (when identified, consider this until proven otherwise)
- Infection (very rare)
- Anything that causes mucosal injury (iatrogenic, medications like steroids)
- Benign etiologies - medications, connective tissue disorders, idiopathic

**Key Takeaways:**
- Recognize what pneumatosis looks like on CT
- Recognize non-enhancing bowel
- Recognize portal venous gas in the liver (peripheral branching) and differentiate from pneumobilia (more central)
- Learn the signs of bowel ischemia

## Case 34: Closed Loop Obstruction (50459)

### Key Findings
- Classic closed loop small bowel obstruction pattern:
  - **Dilated loops proximally**
  - **Abrupt transition point (TP1)**
  - **Re-dilatation** of small bowel distal to transition point
  - **Second transition point (TP2)**, in close proximity to the first
- The intervening segment is at higher risk for strangulation/ischemia
- Some mesenteric stranding and fluid but no late signs of ischemia

**Other signs:** "C shaped" radially distributed closed loop segment, "Double beak sign", "Whirl sign" of twisting mesentery

### Learning Material
**Closed loop obstruction:** obstruction with at least two abrupt transition points, with the segment between having no outlet. Classically treated surgically. The closer together the transition points, the higher the risk of strangulation.

**Causes:** adhesions, volvulus, hernia (including internal hernia)

A large bowel obstruction with a competent ileocecal valve has "closed loop physiology."

**Basic Skill: Following the Bowel** - follow small bowel from proximal to distal in SBO cases.

## Case 35: Intussusception (22132)

### Key Findings
- **GJ Tube** present (proximal end enters stomach, distal tip in jejunum)
- **Bowel within bowel configuration** - also known as the "target sign". The outer wall is the distal recipient loop (intussuscipiens), the inner loop is collapsed within (intussusceptum), and fatty mesentery of the intussusceptum is easy to identify by fatty attenuation.
- **Bowel within bowel** also seen more distally
- Key feature is seeing the mesentery within another loop of bowel

### Learning Material
**Intussusception** refers to a bowel loop that gets pulled into an adjacent loop of bowel.

**Causes in adults:** more commonly a focal lead point - malignant or benign neoplasm, congenital things like Meckel's, inflammation, iatrogenic like J tube. When we see an intussusception in adults, we want to determine if there is an obvious cause on imaging (i.e. presence of a mass).

**Short Length "Transient Intussusception":** It is not uncommon to see short length transient intussusception in the small bowel. This does not cause an obstruction, is idiopathic, and of no clinical significance. There will be no lead point/cause, no obstruction, and it will be short segment (<3.5 cm).

## Case 38: Enteritis (34257)

### Key Findings
- **Duodenal and jejunal thickening** - small bowel thickening/edema is the main finding in enteritis
- **Mesenteric stranding/inflammation** in the jejunal mesentery
- **Small volume free fluid**
- No specific etiology on imaging (no venous thrombus, findings specific for ischemia or IBD)

### Learning Material
**Enteritis** refers to small bowel inflammation. Causes include infectious, inflammatory (IBD), and ischemic etiologies. Less common causes include post-treatment (radiation), graft vs host disease, and vasculitis.

**Imaging Basics: Is the small bowel thick?**
- Single wall thickness of 3+ mm suggests "thickening" but measurements are unreliable when small bowel is underdistended
- Normal jejunal loops have more folds - collapsed normal jejunum looks thicker than ileum
- When assessing small bowel:
  - Mural stratification (submucosal edema) → more confident it is thick
  - Adjacent changes (stranding) → more confident
  - Well distended and appears thick walled → more confident
  - Mural hyperenhancement → more confident
  - If underdistended, no stratification, no adjacent changes → subjective call

## Case 39: Sigmoid Diverticulitis (03281)

### Key Findings
- **Presence of diverticula** (diverticulosis)
- **Sigmoid colonic thickening with adjacent stranding** indicating inflammation
- No complications (no free gas, collection, fistula, thrombophlebitis)

### Learning Material
Colonic diverticula (pseudodiverticula - mucosa/submucosa only) are common. Two main complications:
1. **Diverticulitis** (inflammation)
2. **GI bleed** (from diverticulosis)

Note: Diverticulosis alone can cause "muscular hypertrophy" mimicking colitis/diverticulitis. True diverticulitis shows inflammatory changes (stranding).

**CT in Diverticulitis - to look for complications:**
- Acute Diverticulitis: pericolonic stranding, bowel wall thickening

**Diverticulitis vs Tumor:**
- Colon cancer: more commonly shorter segment involvement
- Abnormal nodes in adjacent mesentery: suggests underlying tumor
- Colonoscopy often suggested after episode resolves to exclude underlying tumor

## Case 41: C Diff Colitis (64245)

### Key Findings
- **Significant colonic thickening**/edema
- **Marked submucosal edema/thickening** effacing mucosal folds - resulting in "**accordion sign**"
- **Pancolonic involvement** including the rectum
- Small to moderate volume **ascites**

When you see very significant thickening/edema like in this case, think of C diff.

### Learning Material
Colitis refers to inflammation of the colon. Common causes are infectious, inflammatory (IBD), and ischemic etiologies.

**Colonic thickening differential:**
- **Ischemia** - tends to spare rectum, distribution depends on cause (watershed areas: splenic flexure, rectosigmoid junction; vascular distribution: SMA or IMA)
- **Inflammatory** - Crohn's: variable, skip involvement, terminal ileitis; UC: continuous from rectum proximal
- **Infectious** - C diff: variable, often very thick like this case; some with right colon predilection (TB, yersinia, amebiasis)
- **Typhlitis (neutropenic enterocolitis)** - in neutropenic patients, involves cecum, ascending colon, distal ileum

**Imaging Basics: Is the colon thick?**
- When well distended, wall should not be > 2 mm in thickness
- If collapsed, generally not > 4 mm single wall thickness
- Gestalt assessment is best; look for stratification, adjacent changes, distended areas still thick

## Case 43: Ischemic Colitis (16647)

### Key Findings
- **Colonic thickening** involving mid transverse to descending colon, worst at **splenic flexure**
- **Loss of mucosal enhancement** at the splenic flexure (compare with sigmoid and transverse)
- No pneumatosis, portal venous gas, perforation

### Learning Material
Classic case of ischemic colitis. Two patterns:
1. **Watershed** - most commonly splenic flexure, also rectosigmoid; occurs in low-flow states, non-occlusive ischemia
2. **Vascular Territory** - in occlusive disease (SMA or IMA territory)

Although lack of mucosal enhancement is confirmatory, you often will NOT see that. Distribution and clinical factors help raise ischemia as a differential. Findings of vascular occlusion, watershed distribution, or later signs (non-enhancement, pneumatosis) are confirmatory.

## Case 44: Meckel's Diverticulitis (08046)

### Key Findings
- **Blind ended diverticulum** in left lower quadrant, arising from small bowel (antimesenteric border). Usually RLQ but position of small bowel is variable.
- **Associated subtle inflammation**

### Learning Material
Subtle case of Meckel's diverticulitis.

**Meckel's Diverticulum:** congenital true diverticulum in distal ileum. "Rule of 2s": present in 2% of people, about 2 feet from IC valve, two types of ectopic tissue (gastric, pancreatic).

**Complications of Meckel's Diverticula:**
- **Bleeding** (usually pediatric, ectopic gastric mucosa - nuclear medicine pertechnetate scan)
- **Meckel's Diverticulitis** (associated with inflammation) - as in this case
- **Small Bowel Obstruction** - due to mesodiverticular band, volvulus, intussusception. SBO in young patient without prior surgery or obvious cause should raise suspicion - look for Meckel's

**Identifying a Meckel's:**
- Often not seen when incidental
- Detection most common with complications
- Blind ended structure arising from distal small bowel
- Other small bowel diverticula can occur; Meckel's more likely if distal ileal, antimesenteric, no other diverticula

## Case 45: Active Upper GI Bleed (35098)

### Key Findings
- Non-contrast: clips in stomach, duodenum, colon. Bowel loops distended with blood; clotted blood more dense.
- On **arterial phase**: contrast (attenuation similar to aorta) within the **duodenal lumen** near the duodenal clip
- On **venous phase**: contrast has pooled significantly (changed in morphology, expanded)

### Learning Material
For active GI bleeds, we look for **active extravasation into the bowel lumen**. We see high attenuation material on arterial phase that was **not present on non-contrast** (confirming it is contrast), that changes morphology/expands on venous/delayed phase.

CT is very sensitive for GI bleeding but only if there is **active/ongoing** bleeding. Sensitivity is ~0.4 mL/min. Protocol requires non-contrast acquisition, arterial phase, and venous/delayed phase.

## Case 50: IgG4 Disease (66221)

- **Biliary dilatation** down to level of pancreatic findings
- **Multifocal bilateral kidney lesions**: non-cystic, inflammatory appearing

**IgG4 related disease** is a systemic autoimmune process resulting in fibro-inflammatory involvement of various tissues. Serum IgG4 levels often elevated.

- Type 2 AIP - only affects pancreas, IgG4 not elevated

## Case 52: Groove Pancreatitis (16085)

### Key Findings
- **Sheet of soft tissue attenuation** centred in pancreaticoduodenal groove
- **Cystic changes in duodenal wall** (Brunner gland hyperplasia) - a key finding
- **Biliary dilatation with stricturing** associated with this process
- Absence of pancreatic duct obstruction

### Learning Material
**Paraduodenal Pancreatitis ("Groove Pancreatitis")** is a **chronic fibro-inflammatory** process centred in the pancreatico-duodenal groove. NOT just acute pancreatitis affecting the groove. Most common cause: alcohol abuse.

You see a fibroinflammatory sheet of tissue centred at the pancreaticoduodenal groove - often only here but can involve adjacent structures. Relatively rare, mimics malignancy.

**⚠️ Always consider malignancy first:**
Soft tissue at this site with biliary/pancreatic duct obstruction should prompt **consideration of malignancy first**. It is more common for malignancy to be incorrectly called groove pancreatitis than the other way around - and this is more dangerous. EUS may be required.

In this case: duodenal wall cysts, lack of pancreatic duct obstruction, and clinical history aligned with paraduodenal pancreatitis.
